Building AI that ships: where it earns its place, and where it doesn't

Jane Doe ·12 Jun 2026 ·8 min read
Hero illustration — the “AI budget” split across product surfaces.

Every founder we talk to this year has the same line in their pitch: “and it's AI-powered.” Sometimes that's the whole product. More often it's a feature looking for a justification. The difference decides whether your AI budget buys something users feel, or a demo that impresses investors and quietly costs you for years.

We've now shipped AI into healthcare triage, fintech fraud checks, and logistics forecasting — and talked roughly as many founders out of it as into it. Here's how we tell the two apart.

Start with the job, not the technology

The useful question is never “where can we add AI?” It's “what job is the user hiring this product to do, and where are they stuck?” AI earns its place when it removes a real bottleneck — reading something messy, predicting something uncertain, generating a first draft of something tedious. If you can't name the bottleneck in a sentence, the AI is decoration.

Where AI earns its place

In practice, the wins cluster around a few shapes of problem:

  • Turning unstructured input — documents, photos, free text — into something your product can act on.
  • Ranking or routing at a scale a person can't keep up with, where “mostly right, instantly” beats “perfect, eventually.”
  • Drafting work a human will still review — replies, summaries, code — so the model saves time without owning the decision.

The best AI features are invisible. The user just notices the product got faster, or stopped asking them to do the boring part.

Where it doesn't

The expensive mistakes look like this: a chatbot bolted onto a product that already had a perfectly good search box; a “smart” recommendation engine trained on three weeks of sparse data; a generative feature shipped without anyone deciding what happens when it's confidently wrong. In all three, a rule, a query, or a well-designed form would have been cheaper, faster, and more trustworthy.

The tell is usually the budget conversation. If the model triples your timeline and the honest answer to “what breaks if we cut it?” is “nothing the user would notice,” cut it — and revisit once the core product has earned the right.

Decision diagram — the build-vs-skip path we walk through during scoping.

How we decide, in practice

During Scoping we put every proposed AI feature through the same three questions: what bottleneck does it remove, what happens when it's wrong, and what's the simplest non-AI version we could ship first? Most features survive in a smaller, sharper form. A few get cut. One or two turn out to be the whole point of the product — and those are the ones worth spending real money on.

None of this needs a research lab. It needs the discipline to spend your AI budget where users feel it, and the honesty to skip it where they wouldn't.

MVP

Minimum Viable Product

The smallest version of your product that still proves the idea with real users — enough to learn whether you're building the right thing, and no more.

In plain terms

An MVP isn't a half-finished product or a cheap one. It's the most focused version you can ship: the one or two things your product absolutely has to do well, built properly, with everything else deliberately left for later. The point isn't to launch something small — it's to learn something real before you've spent your whole budget.

A good MVP answers a specific question: will people actually use this, pay for it, or come back? You can't answer that from a slide deck. You answer it by putting a working slice in front of real users and watching what they do.

Why it matters to you

For a founder, the MVP is the cheapest insurance you can buy against building the wrong thing. Most products that fail don't fail because the code was bad — they fail because nobody wanted what got built. Shipping a focused first version early means you find that out in weeks, not after a year and a full budget.

It also changes the conversation with us. Instead of guessing at a giant scope, we agree on the smallest thing worth shipping, get it in front of users, and let what we learn shape what comes next. That's why every estimate we give starts by asking what the MVP really needs — and what it doesn't.

A quick example

Say you're building a marketplace. The full vision has reviews, messaging, loyalty points, and a mobile app. The MVP might be just this: sellers can list, buyers can pay, and money changes hands safely. Ship that, see if the two sides actually transact, and you've learned the one thing that decides whether the rest is worth building.
